Written by the Author

I'm 16. I Wrote a Book About Why We Hate Each Other.

Zeyvian Lee  ·  Lubbock, Texas  ·  Author, Division, by America

Let me ask you something. When was the last time you were genuinely surprised by a politician choosing love over hate? When did you last see someone on the opposite side of the aisle treated like a neighbor instead of an enemy? If you're struggling to remember — that's exactly what my book is about.

I'm Zeyvian Lee. I'm 16 years old. I live in Lubbock, Texas. And I wrote a book called Division, by America.

Not because I had all the answers. But because I couldn't stop asking the question: Why does America feel like it's at war with itself? And more importantly — who's actually winning that war?

The answer I found wasn't the one most people want to hear. We're not divided because Democrats hate Republicans. We're not divided because of social media, or culture wars, or religion. We're divided because division has always been useful to the people with the most power — and costly to everyone else. That pattern started in the 1600s with the invention of "race" as a political tool to keep poor people from recognizing their shared condition. It never stopped. The faces change. The platforms go digital. But the strategy is the same: convince people their enemy is their neighbor, not the system above them.

That's what my book is about. And here's what happened when I started taking it seriously.

The Encounter

He Said He Was Tired of Being Told to Hate His Neighbor.

The first time I came across James Talarico, he was hosting a panel in Lubbock about school vouchers. I wasn't there because of the politics — I was there because of what he represented to me: someone actually trying to have a real conversation across the divide. After the event, I shared my message with him. At that point it wasn't a book yet — it was a paper, an essay, a set of ideas I'd been building. He stopped and listened. He told me it was a great idea. He even gave me a direct way to stay in touch, and we spoke a few more times after that.

The next time I saw him was this past February. He remembered me — not fully, I could tell — but he was the same person. Still choosing something different. That photo above is from that day.

James Talarico is now the Texas Democratic nominee for U.S. Senate — one of the most closely watched races in the country in 2026. Former middle school teacher. Eighth-generation Texan. Presbyterian seminarian. And quoted in my book — not because I set up some important meeting, but because what he said to a crowd in Lubbock one afternoon was the truest thing I'd heard from anyone in politics:

"I am tired of being pitted against my neighbor.
I am tired of being told to hate my neighbor."
— James Talarico, quoted in Division, by America

Since I put that quote in the book, the world has added its own context. This past week, Pete Hegseth's pastor — a close spiritual adviser to the Secretary of Defense — went on a podcast and said he wanted James Talarico "crucified with Christ." The podcast host said he "prays that God kills him."

Talarico's response to a man publicly praying for his death was not a press release. Not a fundraiser. Not a campaign ad.

"Jesus loves. Christian Nationalism kills. You may pray for my death, Pastor, but I still love you. I love you more than you could ever hate me."
— James Talarico, on X, March 2026

Read that again. A man in a government adviser's orbit publicly called for a politician's death — and that politician responded with love. Not strategic love. Not performed love. The kind that costs you something. The kind that proves a point no speech ever could.

That is what my book is trying to say. Not that division isn't real. But that the way we respond to it matters. And the people who choose love when hatred is being broadcast at them from the highest levels of government — those are the ones actually living the answer.

Across the Aisle

A Republican Said the Same Thing. From the Other Wing.

James Fishback — Republican Candidate for Florida Governor

My book doesn't only quote Democrats. One of the most striking lines I came across came from a Republican running for governor of Florida. Speaking to hundreds of college students at the University of Florida, he said something that stopped the room:

"There's a right wing, there is a left wing — but both wings are attached to the same bird as the Uniparty, which is flying in the wrong direction."
— James Fishback, UF Town Hall, March 11, 2026

A Republican candidate telling his own party they're flying in the wrong direction. A Democratic candidate telling his would-be executioners he loves them. Two different wings. The same diagnosis. That's not a coincidence. That's the argument my book makes in Chapter 3: the real division in America has never been left versus right. It's always been power versus everyone else.
